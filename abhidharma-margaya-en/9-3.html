3. Meaning of Dependent Origination Pali

share

Lesson 2

  1. Conditioned by ignorance, formations arise,
  2. Conditioned by formations, consciousness arises,
  3. Conditioned by consciousness, mentality-materiality arises,
  4. Conditioned by mentality-materiality, the sixfold base arises,
  5. Conditioned by the sixfold base, contact arises,
  6. Conditioned by contact, feeling arises,
  7. Conditioned by feeling, craving arises,
  8. Conditioned by craving, clinging arises,
  9. Conditioned by clinging, becoming arises,
  10. Conditioned by becoming, birth arises,
  11. Conditioned by birth, aging, death, sorrow, lamentation, pain, displeasure, and despair arise.

Thus is the origin of this whole mass of suffering.

From here onwards, these clauses showing Dependent Origination will be explained in order.

Avijjā paccayā saṅkhārā

Conditioned by ignorance, formations arise.

Here, ignorance is the delusion that covers the four facts: suffering, the cause of suffering, the cessation of suffering, and the path leading to the cessation of suffering which is Nirvana.

'Formations' (Sankhara) are mundane wholesome and unwholesome volitions. The group of wholesome and unwholesome volitions is divided into three parts: meritorious formations (puññābhisankhāra), demeritorious formations (apuññābhisankhāra), and imperturbable formations (āneñjābhisankhāra). The eight sense-sphere wholesome volitions and the five fine-material sphere wholesome volitions are called meritorious formations. The twelve unwholesome volitions are called demeritorious formations. The four immaterial sphere wholesome volitions are called imperturbable formations. Since the immaterial sphere wholesome state associated with strong concentration is not easily shaken, it is called imperturbable formation. Formations are also threefold as bodily formations, verbal formations, and mental formations.

The person with ignorance, wrongly understanding this Samsara, which is a mass of suffering, as happiness, performs various evil deeds to maintain his existence and for gratification. He performs meritorious deeds for happiness in future lives. If there were no ignorance, he would not do those merits and demerits. Therefore, it is said that formations arise conditioned by ignorance.

Among these ultimate realities, there is no formation that arises from a single cause. Whatever arises, arises from many causes. In this Dependent Origination, only one dharma, ignorance, is shown as the cause of formations to indicate the principal cause. In other places too, a single cause is shown to indicate the principal cause.

A question may arise here: how can wholesome states, which are opposed to it, arise from delusion which is definitely unwholesome? There is no rule that cause and effect must be similar. From mud that is foul-smelling and discolored, a lotus that is fragrant and colorful arises. From fire that is very bright, discolored smoke arises. Therefore, it should be accepted that wholesome states can also arise from unwholesome states which are opposed to wholesome states.

Saṅkhāra paccayā viññāṇaṃ

Conditioned by formations, consciousness arises.

The consciousness that arises conditioned by formations is the thirty-two mundane resultant cittas. Supramundane resultants are not taken here. Mundane resultants are also of two parts: rebirth resultants and life-continuum resultants. Among formations, the volition associated with restlessness (uddhacca) is not a condition for rebirth resultants. The direct knowledge (abhinna) volition is not a condition for any resultant consciousness. It was stated in the fifth chapter that volition associated with restlessness does not give rebirth results. You can know about direct knowledge volition from the section on meditation subjects.

The arising of resultant consciousness conditioned by formations is a deep point. If these resultant consciousnesses arose without kamma, then every resultant consciousness should arise for everyone. Resultant consciousnesses related to specific kammas do not arise for others, but only for those who performed those kammas. It should be understood from this that if there is no kamma, resultant consciousness does not arise. Since resultant consciousnesses arise only when there is kamma, it can be known that kamma is a condition for resultant consciousnesses.

Viññāṇa paccayā nāmarūpaṃ

Conditioned by consciousness, mentality-materiality arises.

The 'mentality' (nama) that arises from the condition of consciousness are the cetasikas that arise associated with those respective consciousnesses. The fifty-two cetasikas and the materiality born of the four conditions such as kamma are the mentality-materiality that arise from the condition of consciousness. The mentality-materiality that can arise due to specific consciousnesses should be understood according to the facts stated separately in the previous chapters.

Only resultant consciousnesses arise from the condition of formations. Wholesome, unwholesome, and functional consciousnesses also become conditions for mentality-materiality. Therefore, it should be known that the consciousness arising from the condition of formations is resultant consciousness, and the consciousness that is a condition for mentality-materiality includes all resultant and non-resultant consciousnesses.

Rebirth consciousness is a condition only for mentality and kamma-born materiality. Life-continuum consciousness is a condition for mentality and all materiality.

The arising of mentality-materiality from the condition of consciousness should be understood according to the planes of existence (bhava). In the sense and fine-material planes, both mentality and materiality arise from the condition of consciousness. In the immaterial plane, only mentality arises from the condition of consciousness. In the non-percipient plane, there is no consciousness. There, only materiality arises by the power of the kamma consciousness belonging to the past life.

If consciousness does not arise, there is no arising of mentality-materiality. From this, it can be known that the arising of mentality-materiality is due to consciousness. Just as when fire arises, light arises from it, it should be known that when consciousness arises, mentality-materiality also arises from it.

Nāmarūpa paccayā saḷāyatanaṃ

Conditioned by mentality-materiality, the sixfold base arises.

It has been stated above that there are twelve bases (ayatanas). Of those twelve bases, the six bases taken here are the eye base, ear base, nose base, tongue base, body base, and mind base.

The word 'nama' refers to the three ultimate realities: citta, cetasika, and Nirvana. Here, 'nama' refers to the fifty-two cetasikas. 'Rupa' refers to the eleven materialities belonging to a single continuity: the four great elements, the six physical bases, and the life faculty. In some books, twelve materialities are taken including nutritive essence.

The arising of the sixfold base from the condition of mentality-materiality should also be understood analytically. Since there is no materiality in the immaterial plane, only the mind base arises there conditioned by mentality alone. In the sense plane, all six bases arise conditioned by both mentality and materiality. In the fine-material plane, three bases - eye base, ear base, and mind base - arise conditioned by both mentality and materiality. In the non-percipient plane, not even one of these six bases arises. The five physical bases such as the eye, which belong to the sixfold base, are included in the condition group here because the mind base arises due to the physical bases. The arising of the sixfold base is simply the development of mentality-materiality. A small fruit does not have a shell, kernel, or water. As it grows, they arise. The arising of the sixfold base of mentality-materiality should be understood in the same way.

Saḷāyatana paccayā phasso

Conditioned by the sixfold base, contact arises.

Here, 'contact' (phassa) is the contact cetasika. It is sixfold as eye-contact, ear-contact, nose-contact, tongue-contact, body-contact, and mind-contact. By saying contact arises due to the sixfold base, it should not be taken that all six contacts arise from all six bases, or that six contacts arise from each base. Only eye-contact arises from the eye base. From each base such as the ear, only one contact such as ear-contact arises. In the immaterial world, only mind-contact arises. In the fine-material world, three contacts arise. In the sense world, all six contacts arise.

When the six internal bases exist, collision with external bases occurs at some time. If there are no internal bases, those collisions never occur. Therefore, the existence of the six bases is the cause for the arising of contact.

Phassa paccayā vedanā.

Conditioned by contact, feeling arises.

Since contact is a universal cetasika, it exists in all cittas. All of them are conditions for feelings. However, in this discourse on Dependent Origination, only the contact existing in the thirty-two mundane resultant cittas is taken. Although feeling exists in every citta, only the thirty-two mundane resultant feelings are taken here.

If there is contact, feeling will also arise. If there is no contact, feeling will never arise. For feeling to arise, there must be contact. Therefore, it is said that conditioned by contact, feeling arises.

Vedanā paccayā taṇhā

Conditioned by feeling, craving arises.

Here, as the feeling that conditions craving, all eighty-one feelings associated with the eighty-one mundane cittas are taken into account. Although craving caused by feeling is generally one, it is sixfold according to objects: craving for forms, craving for sounds, craving for smells, craving for tastes, craving for tangibles, and craving for mental objects. Since each craving is threefold as craving for sense pleasures, craving for existence, and craving for non-existence, there are eighteen cravings. Since they are found in both internal and external continuities, there are thirty-six cravings by way of continuity. Since each craving is threefold by way of the three times - past, future, and present - there are one hundred and eight cravings.

Since beings like pleasant feeling, it is obvious that craving arises regarding it. Painful feeling is something beings dislike. A question arises here: how does painful feeling, which is a disliked thing, become a cause for craving? It is true that beings dislike painful feeling. A being oppressed by painful feeling desires happiness. The cause for that was the painful feeling that arose in him. Therefore, it should be understood that painful feeling also becomes a cause for craving. Since neutral feeling is peaceful, beings like it too. Therefore, all three feelings are taken as causes for craving.

Taṇhā paccayā upādānaṃ

Conditioned by craving, clinging arises.

It was stated in the seventh chapter that there are four clingings. When taken as dharmas, there are two clingings: craving and wrong view. The difference between craving arising conditioned by feeling and clinging-craving is as follows: craving arising from the condition of feeling is weak. Clinging-craving arising from the condition of craving is strong. Craving arising from the condition of feeling should be understood like the craving arising in a gem miner before he finds a gem. Clinging-craving should be understood like the craving arising regarding a valuable gem that has come into his hand.

Craving arises conditioned by feeling for everyone, both Buddhists and non-Buddhists. From craving, clinging to sense pleasures arises. Due to craving, clinging to the doctrine of self also arises for everyone, both Buddhists and non-Buddhists. Clinging to views and clinging to rites and rituals cannot arise for true Buddhists.

Clinging also arises from the development of craving that arises due to feeling.

Upādāna paccayā bhavo.

Conditioned by clinging, becoming arises.

There are two becomings arising from the condition of clinging: kamma becoming (kamma bhava) and rebirth becoming (upapatti bhava). The group of mundane wholesome and unwholesome volitions is called 'kamma becoming'. The group of dharmas consisting of the twelve unwholesome cittas, seventeen mundane wholesome cittas, and fifty-two cetasikas is also called 'kamma becoming'. The group of dharmas consisting of the thirty-two mundane resultants, the thirty-three cetasikas associated with them, and the twenty kamma-born materialities is called 'rebirth becoming'.

Nine rebirth becomings are also shown: sense becoming, fine-material becoming, immaterial becoming, percipient becoming, non-percipient becoming, neither-perception-nor-non-perception becoming, one-constituent becoming, four-constituent becoming, and five-constituent becoming. Sense becoming etc. are obvious. Non-percipient becoming, which has only one aggregate called the materiality aggregate, is called one-constituent becoming. Immaterial becoming, which has only the four mental aggregates, is called four-constituent becoming. All remaining becomings that have all five aggregates are called five-constituent becoming.

When clingings have arisen, due to them, one begins to do various things to maintain the five aggregates which one takes as 'I' and 'mine', to make them happy, and to obtain the five aggregates in the future. Those things done are the kamma becoming arising from the condition of clinging. Rebirth becoming does not arise directly due to clinging. It arises due to kamma becoming. The cause of rebirth becoming is kamma becoming. The cause of kamma becoming is clinging. Saying that rebirth becoming arises from the condition of clinging is by taking the cause producing kamma becoming as the cause of rebirth becoming as well. In the world, the cause of a cause is also sometimes spoken of as the cause. 'Rice is cooked with firewood' is such a place. Rice is cooked not by firewood but by fire. Firewood is the cause of fire. However, firewood, which is the cause of fire, is spoken of as the cause of rice. Saying that rebirth becoming arises from the condition of clinging should also be understood in that way.

Bhava paccayā jāti

Conditioned by becoming, birth arises.

Here, 'birth' refers to the aggregates that first arise for beings in those respective existences. Of the two becomings, kamma becoming and rebirth becoming, only kamma becoming is a condition for birth. Therefore, although both kamma becoming and rebirth becoming are taken by the word 'bhava' in 'Upādāna paccayā bhavo', only kamma becoming should be taken by the word 'bhava' in 'Bhava paccayā jāti'. That kamma becoming is a condition for birth can be understood from the differences in beings. Twin children born from the same mother's womb at the same time and raised together differ from each other in happiness and suffering etc. because of kamma.

The 'sankhara' mentioned in 'Avijjā paccayā saṅkhārā' and this 'kamma bhava' are similar as kamma. Three existences are connected to this teaching of Dependent Origination. By the name 'sankhara', the past kamma that was the cause for the birth of the present existence is expressed. By the name 'kamma bhava', the kamma performed in the present existence is expressed.

Jāti paccayā jarā maraṇaṃ soka parideva dukkha domanassupāyāsā sambhavanti.

If birth occurs, aging and death inevitably occur. Sorrow, lamentation, pain, displeasure, and despair also occur for many beings. Sorrow etc. occur less for some beings and more for some beings. They do not exist in the fine-material and immaterial worlds. Sorrow etc., which do not occur for everyone who has birth, are included here and preached to show the evil of the round of rebirths.

Here, 'aging' is the static phase approaching the dissolution of mentality-materiality. 'Death' is the dissolution of mentality-materiality, the destruction. 'Sorrow' is the unpleasant feeling arising due to loss of relatives etc. 'Lamentation' is the wailing sound made by those stricken with sorrow due to loss of relatives etc. 'Pain' is bodily painful feeling. 'Displeasure' is mental painful feeling. 'Despair' is the great exhaustion arising in the mind due to loss of relatives etc. It is the hatred cetasika itself arising in a certain way.
