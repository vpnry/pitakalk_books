1. Section on Conditions

share

The group of ultimate realities (paramartha dharma) consisting of the eighty-nine cittas, fifty-two cetasikas, and twenty-eight rupas, which are the fundamental elements forming the multitude of beings such as devas and humans and all objects, has been analyzed and shown in various ways in the previous seven chapters. Now, what you should investigate is this matter: do those ultimate realities arise spontaneously? Or are they created by some powerful being? How do they arise?

Citta, cetasika, and rupa paramarthas arise from various causes. Therefore, they are called 'conditioned phenomena' (sankhata dharma) and also 'formations' (sankhara). The Nirvana paramartha is not something that arises from conditions. Therefore, it is called the 'unconditioned element' (asankhata dhatu).

The causes that produce conditioned phenomena are also called 'conditions' (paccaya). The Buddha has preached the causes of the arising of formations in three methods: the method of Noble Truths, the method of Dependent Origination (Paticcasamuppada), and the method of Relations (Patthana). Of these, the method of Noble Truths is the very brief method. The method of Dependent Origination is the medium method. The method of Relations is the detailed method.

Since the analysis of Noble Truths was stated in the seventh chapter, what remains to be explained in this section on conditions are the method of Dependent Origination and the method of Relations.

The method of Dependent Origination is the cause-and-effect method that teaches how samsara continues unbroken by the arising of other dharmas due to the presence of certain dharmas, such as: when ignorance exists, formations arise due to ignorance; when formations exist, consciousness arises due to them, and so on. It has been preached in the Vibhanga Prakarana of the Abhidharma Pitaka.

The cause-and-effect method preached in the Patthana Maha Prakarana is the method of Relations. In the teaching of Dependent Origination, only the arising of formations etc. due to ignorance etc. is taught; there is no teaching that ignorance has these specific powers, and that ignorance becomes a condition for formations or produces formations by such and such powers among those powers in such and such ways. However, in the Patthana, the conditioning powers existing in all dharmas, both ultimate and non-ultimate, and how those dharmas become conditions for other dharmas by various powers such as hetu (root), arammana (object), etc., are taught. A complete knowledge of the Buddha's Dhamma is gained by learning both these methods. Of these two methods, the shorter method is the method of Dependent Origination.
